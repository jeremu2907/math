RF Transmission Range Estimation

Consider the following scenario. An RX SDR is receiving a signal transmission from a distance rrr. We are interested in estimating rrr. However, SDRs only provide IQ samples which contains no TX information. Furthermore, the IQ samples are relative to the SDR hardware which does not give us any real, absolute scalar values to work with.

This article will explore a limited method to estimate rrr given the constraints described above.

RX denotes receiver.
TX denotes transmitter.

Power Loss in Free Space

RF transmission power decrease is characterized by the inverse square law. The same transmission power ppp at a distance rrr is
p(r)=PD1D2(c4πrf)2p(r) = P D_1D_2\left( \frac{c}{4 \pi r f} \right)^2p(r)=PD1​D2​(4πrfc​)2

Where PPP is the TX transmission power, DiD_iDi​ are antenna directivity values, ccc is the speed of light, fff is the transmission frequency, and rrr is the RX distance from TX. This is the standard Free Space Path Loss (FSPL) equation.

BEGIN VERY IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER

Because the SDR front-end contains nonlinear analog components (LNA, mixer, and ADC), the measured IQ power does not scale perfectly linearly with the RX voltage2^22, which is implied above.

For the specific hardware configuration used in this experiment, the empirical data is well approximated by a quadratic regression:

Vantenna2≈aρ2+bρ+cV_{\text{antenna}}^2 \approx a\rho^2 + b\rho + cVantenna2​≈aρ2+bρ+c

This polynomial relationship should therefore be interpreted as an empirical calibration model specific to the measurement setup, rather than a fundamental physical law.

The coefficients of this regression are dependent on the SDR hardware, receiver gain settings, center frequency, and bandwidth. Changing any of these parameters requires recalibrating the mapping function.

Pitfall

Because of the regression that is based on real-world observation, the graph does not agree completely with theoretical models. For example, in the setup above, a finite maximum occurs at ρ≈0.53292\rho \approx 0.53292ρ≈0.53292 even though the graph should extend to infinity. Theoretically, lim⁡ρ→0+r(ρ)=∞\lim_{\rho \rightarrow 0^+} r(\rho) = \infinlimρ→0+​r(ρ)=∞ but r^(ρ)\hat{r}(\rho)r^(ρ) does not behave the same way. This method of estimation should be used with consideration and complemented by safeguards, given the limitation explained above.
